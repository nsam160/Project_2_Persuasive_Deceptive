Proposition:
Rising female teritary enrollment over time show decline in population growth?

FOR the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Selective Annotation (-0.5): Highlight only the points where a clear trend downward in the fluctuating population decline occur
  • Manipulating Data To Fit on Same Graph (1): One line have values ranging to 100%, while the others stayed within the 1% range, therefore the line with wider percentage range was transformed to proportion
  • Effective Colors (2): Chose pink to represent female and a more neutral black color to represent the population
  • Effective Axes Range (2): Stretch the graph wide to show changes are gradual over time, not steep

For this design, I went with a straightforward, trend line graph, with some annotation highlighting certain trends. The reason I went with this design is because it makes it most easy for audience to understand the message and it gets the point across for my proposition. I've attempted to use a storytelling method as with the against proposition, but the feedback I've recieved from it was not so great as many said it was confusing, rather than persuasive. Other graphs that I attempted were bar graphs, scatterplot, and boxplot, but they didn't work out because they were either too messy, like the scatterplot, showing no clear trends, or the graph does not show correlation well as it was on separate sub-figures.

AGAINST the Proposition

Design Decisions and Rationale:

  • Telling A Roundabout Story (-1.5): Involve other variables outside the ones that directly answer the question
  • Questionable Variable Choice for Population Growth (-2): Chose absolute growth from a starting date rather than percent growth annually to show higher increase than actual growth
  • Effective Color Choice (2): Choose non-greyscale color to show important variables to highlight, pink to represent female, and blue to represent world population
  • Data Transformation For Population Change (2): Took the total population of every country for every year and subtract it to the population of that country in 1970
  • Data Binning (-0.5): Datas were binned, through decades rather than a 1 year or 5 year mark, to show tremendous difference across categories

For this design, I went with a storytelling approach to make the design most believable. If I use the same method as my for proposition, audience will most likely look suspiciously at this graph because the same data have to be used. Therefore, I must include reasons as to why it is possible that population grow overall. Although I mentioned before that the downside of the storytelling method is that it is confusing, making audience have doubts, it may be the best case here because to disprove something, we always need an explanation as to why it is wrong. One downside of this method is that direct correlation between variables are not plotted, but their relationships are shown in years. Other alternatives that I considered to fix the correlation problem was scatterplot, but it looked too messy and the trends were not that clear, so I just stick with the barplot to show the clear trends.

Final Reflection

Across these two visualization, I merged 3 datasets, which are gender, climate, and health, from the World Bank dataset to get all the information that I needed. Then, I filtered out the rows to only include years after 1970. This is because I noticed that data are most likely to be missing across all the variables used if they are between 1960 and 1969. After that, I filtered the columns to only include the necessary variables, like year, country name, morality rate, population growth rate, population amount, and rate of female in higher education. Noticing that many columns are missing a lot of values, I decided to group by the country name, filtered out the countries with less than 30 values overall (60% of the data) per columns of information, and perform regression imputation on the rest of the data. The value 30 was chosen so we can still have as much data as possible, while having enough data to fit a regression model to it without having too many errors. The regression model was automatically chosen from 4 possible models, linear, exponential, logarithmic, and quadratic, where the one with the lowest RMSE value is chosen. Regression imputation was performed separately on different columns groupby country name. After filling in missing data using the best regression model, only basic data transformation was performed, like conversion from percent to proportion or making large numbers to be in units of millions. Finally, before putting it into the final graphs, data were averaged out by groups of years or decades.

The most difficult part of this project was to prove something that is not true. I considered this to be difficult because we would either need a deep understanding of the data we are dealing with in order to filter it a certain way or we would have to spend days finding out where data can be manipulated. Additionally, somewhere in making this project, I got too immersed into making my visualization most believable from both sides that I forgot the basic visualization rules, simplicity. What was most straightforward in this project was coming up with a proposition. Although coming up with proposition takes time to explore data to create a final one, it was the most simple step out of the entire project because I had a basic idea of what I was looking for and I had prior knowledge to things going on around the world. What surprised me in this project was how easily data can argue both sides of an argument depending on how a person use it.

How I define 'ethical analysis and visualization' is to take into consideration data transparency or any other moral reponsibility throughout the process of analyzing and visualizing data. Any data manipulation must be reported and potential harm the visualization can make, must have disclaimers. However, otherwise, 'acceptable' persuasive techniques, like graph annotation is encouraged. The bounds that I draw between 'acceptable' persuasive choices vs. 'misleading' ones all comes down to whether the lies is obvious or is it well-hidden behind other data that audience deemed trustworthy. For example, simplifying complex information for clarity, as I did with the mean, choosing color to draw attention to details, as I did with pink, and highlighting important pattern without filtering underlying data, as I did with the annotation, can all be deemed as 'acceptable' persuasive choices. Examples of 'misleading' ones can include exaggerating trends by truncating axes or over filtering data to only show a clear trends and any other deviation from the trends are hidden.
